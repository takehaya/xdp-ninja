# English Academic Paper Review

English counterpart of `academic_writing_skills.md` (`review-academic-paper-jp`).
Use it on the English `.tex` prose. After fixing the English source, mirror any
content-level change back into the Japanese draft.

This guide covers conventions specific to an English academic paper. For general
English prose (cognitive load, viewpoint, cutting LLM-isms), use the
`english-tech-writing` skill; do not duplicate its rules here. The norms below are
English-native, not translated from the Japanese guide: English science writing
uses semicolons, colons, and calibrated hedges that the Japanese guide cuts.

## Workflow

1. Identify the target passage.
2. Diagnose by principle (report which principles the prose violates).
3. Rewrite the whole passage from scratch (do not propose sentence-level patches).
4. Self-scan with quotations, then self-correct.
5. Write the result to a review memo. Do not edit the paper source; the author
   applies it after review.

## Principles

Do not patch the original sentence by sentence. Rewrite the whole passage while
preserving its meaning, and revise the subsection structure when it helps.

### Register and style

- Formal academic register throughout. No contractions: write "do not", "it is",
  "cannot", not "don't", "it's", "can't".
- Remove conversational vocabulary and filler: "a lot of", "kind of", "pretty",
  "stuff", "basically", "actually", "of course", "and so on" as a catch-all.
- No sentence fragments used for effect.

#### Tense

- In evaluation and experiment sections, report results in the **past tense**.
  Measurements, confirmations, comparisons, and observations go to the past
  ("We measured ...", "The verifier accepted the bytecode on six kernels",
  "F5 matched and rejected the intended packets"), and an end-of-subsection
  summary closes in the past as well ("We confirmed that ...", "the bound held").
- A present-tense framing verb for a completed measurement also goes to the past:
  "We evaluate / report / compare / generate" describing what the experiment did
  becomes "We evaluated / reported / compared / generated".
- Keep the **present tense** for pure navigation ("the following sections
  show ..."), general truths ("pcap-filter has no syntax for nested
  encapsulation", "the verifier differs across kernel versions"), and metric
  definitions.
- Stating the paper's own claim is conventionally present ("We present", "This
  paper describes"), but the measured result inside that clause stays past:
  "We show that the bytecode **was** accepted across six kernels", not
  "is accepted".
- Do not flip tense for the same class of statement within a passage. If two
  sentences both report measured outcomes, both are past.
- Failure flagged in review: an Abstract or Introduction that reports results in
  the present tense, "The generated bytecode **is** accepted ... **matches** and
  **rejects** the intended packets", "We show that filters spanning ...". Decide
  the policy once and apply it; mixed tense reads as unintentional.

#### Connectives

- Avoid the conversational `, so`. It is casual and not an academic register.
  - Split into two sentences with "Therefore,", "As a result,", or "Hence,":
    "... the offset is not known until run time. As a result, a naive lowering
    reads at a position the verifier cannot prove in bounds."
  - Or restructure with "because" / "since": "Because the offset is not known
    until run time, a naive lowering reads ...".
- The same caution applies to other clause-chaining fillers ("and so", "plus",
  "also" as a sentence opener).

#### Parentheticals

- Parentheses are normal in English; do not ban them. The rule is to keep
  load-bearing content out of them, so the sentence still reads if the
  parenthetical is removed.
  - Avoid hiding a definition or a necessary qualification inside `()`:
    "We use the term verifier-safe in the sense of Solleza et al. (a well-formed
    filter should compile to bytecode the verifier accepts)" buries the
    definition. Promote it with a colon or a full sentence: "... in the sense of
    Solleza et al.: a well-formed filter should compile to bytecode the verifier
    accepts."
  - A short, genuinely parenthetical aside is acceptable: "(the Wireshark display
    filter is one)" reads fine. Keep it only if the example earns its place;
    otherwise fold it into the clause.
- A bare cross-reference glued to a claim, such as "(§6)" in "not as a formal
  guarantee (§6)", is often deletable. Check whether the claim still reads without
  it.
- Do not stack several parentheticals in one sentence; that is where the prose
  turns choppy.
- Semicolons and colons are legitimate academic punctuation; use them correctly
  rather than avoiding them. Do not use `**` bold for emphasis in prose. Per the
  project rule, do not use the em-dash `---` in prose; the range dash (6.1--7.0)
  is fine. [[feedback_no_em_dash]]

### Academic writing (clear subject-verb-object)

Write sentences in which these are unambiguous:

1. Subject (who / what)
2. Object (what is acted on)
3. Verb (a concrete action)

- Make the author the subject ("We") for author actions; make the system the
  subject for system behavior.
- Use verbs for which "subject does what to object" is explicit. Avoid vague
  verbs ("connect", "be orthogonal to", "deal with", "handle", "leverage") when
  they hide the action.
- No wishful or subjective phrasing: "we want to", "it would be desirable",
  "demand is growing" → "we need to", "X is required".
- State a fact as a fact when the evidence supports it; drop empty
  throat-clearing such as "it can be said that". But do not strip calibrated
  hedges. English scientific writing wants the claim to match the evidence, and
  over-claiming is the worse error: keep "these results suggest", "may", and
  "we argue that" when the evidence is partial.
- Replace vague magnitude words ("major", "representative", "various") with
  examples or numbers.
- Check borrowed idioms and clichés ("source of truth", "first-class",
  "seamlessly", "robust", "battle-tested", "production-grade", "facilitate")
  against their literal sense. Cut them if removing them loses no information.
- Make an *empty* hedge concrete rather than deleting the caution:
  - Numeric: "mostly correct" → "correct in M of N cases".
  - Comparison: "relatively fast" → "within the same range as implementation A".
  - Unsupported absolute: "tends to be X" → "is X" only when measured; otherwise
    keep the hedge and name the evidence.

### Paragraph writing

- Open each paragraph with an umbrella topic sentence that states what the
  paragraph is about.
- A run of one-to-two-sentence paragraphs, or one oversized paragraph, signals a
  bad topic split. Re-segment to an appropriate granularity.
- Use a transition word when giving an example ("For example,", "For instance,").

### Section structure

- Close each subsection with a concluding sentence.
- A section opening may navigate its subsections. Otherwise, do not state the
  same content twice in one section.

### No preemptive defense

Do not scatter negations that pre-empt a misreading ("X is not a contribution",
"the point is not ...", "the focus is not X").

- State scope once, in Limitations.
- Do not leave editorial meta-commentary in the body ("we keep this minimal so
  the focus does not shift").
- Do not pre-empt a misreading nobody would have.

### Abstract

- Three paragraphs (problem / proposal / results and conclusion); about 150-250
  words for a workshop paper.
- Self-contained: do not use a label first defined in the body.
- Necessary and sufficient: no proper nouns that need not be named, no numbers
  with no point.
- No monospace code, inline or block.
- Report results and the conclusion in the past tense (per the tense rule above).
- Connect the closing sentence to the results rather than jumping into it cold:
  "These results show that Kunai enables ...", not a bare "Kunai enables ..."
  dropped after a list of findings with no connective.

### Abstract conclusion and the Conclusion section

Typical structure:

> We [showed / presented / realized] X. This enabled Y.

Requirements:

- Close in the past tense. Present-tense capability claims ("can execute",
  "supports") belong in the proposal paragraph, not the conclusion.
- Pair with the Background: the problem posed in the background and the
  resolution claimed in the conclusion should correspond as noun phrases of the
  same scope.

## Self-scan (quoted, per principle)

After producing the rewrite, quote from the rewrite for each principle and check
it against that principle. A bare "pass" label is not allowed.

## Output format

Write the review to a separate memo file (for example under `review_memo/`); do
not edit the `.tex`. The memo must let a reviewer see what changes and why.

Present each proposed edit as a three-part block, in this order:

1. **Item** — a heading with the `file:line` anchor, the violated principle, and
   severity.
2. **Diff** — a fenced code block tagged `diff`: a `-` line is the current text, a
   `+` line is the proposal, and an unchanged context line is prefixed with a
   space so the change is anchored in its surroundings. Show only the lines that
   move plus a line or two of context.
3. **Reason** — one or two sentences on why the change holds and that the meaning
   is preserved.

Then, with no diff:

- the principles that passed (what you chose not to change, and why), and
- un-reviewed candidates (location only), never a silent bulk edit.

The whole memo follows this skeleton:

1. Diagnosis by principle (only the violated principles).
2. Rewrite plan (short: what to keep, what to cut).
3. Per-item blocks (Item / Diff / Reason), one per proposed edit.
4. Quoted self-scan.
5. Re-rewrite based on the scan.

## Cautions

- Do not edit the paper source directly. Silently changing the `.tex` is unkind to
  a reviewer, who can no longer see what moved. Write findings to a memo and let
  the author apply them.
- Do not bulk-replace un-reviewed occurrences of a pattern (for example, every
  `, so`). Run each instance through the workflow; record the rest as candidates,
  with locations only.
- If the original meaning is unclear, ask the user; do not guess.
- When a rewrite needs a decision first (a section's premise moves, or a
  contribution claim changes), ask the user.
